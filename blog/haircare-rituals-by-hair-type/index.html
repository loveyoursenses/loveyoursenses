Hair Care Rituals by Hair Type: Fine, Curly, Thick and Colour-Treated

18 July 2026 · 6 minute read

A washing schedule that leaves fine hair looking fresh will leave curly hair dry and frizzy within days, and the daily rinse that keeps thick hair manageable would strip colour from treated hair far faster than it needed to fade. Most hair care advice online is written as though hair is one substance with one correct routine, and that is precisely why so much of it fails to work for so many people.

Hair type changes almost everything about what a routine should look like, how often to wash, what to layer on afterward, even the order products go on in. Start from your actual hair rather than a generic routine and the guesswork mostly disappears.

Why one routine was never going to fit everyone

Fine hair, curly hair, thick hair and colour-treated hair each have a different relationship with the same core variable, oil. Fine hair shows oil buildup fastest because there is less hair to distribute it along. Curly and coily hair struggles to move oil down the strand at all, since the natural spiral shape slows its journey from scalp to tip, which is a large part of why curly hair often looks and feels drier than straight hair even when scalp oil production is identical. Thick hair takes longer for the same oil to reach the ends simply because there is more hair for it to travel through. Colour treatment changes the hair's structure directly, making it more porous and often more prone to dryness regardless of natural type. Once you see washing frequency and product choice as answers to that one variable, the differences between hair types stop feeling arbitrary.

Find your type

Fine or Straight Hair

Wash · Every One to Two Days

Fine hair shows oil and loses volume fastest, so more frequent washing with a lightweight shampoo keeps it looking fresh rather than flat. The trap to avoid is heavy, richly moisturising products, which weigh fine hair down quickly, so lighter formulas and a light hand with conditioner, focused mainly on the ends rather than the roots, tend to work best.

Curly or Coily Hair

Wash · Every Three to Seven Days

Because oil struggles to travel down a curved strand, curly and coily hair generally needs washing far less often than straight hair, and frequent shampooing tends to strip moisture the hair badly needs to hold. A rich conditioner, generous and left on for a few minutes, does much of the real work here, and many curly types benefit from co-washing, cleansing with conditioner alone, between full shampoo washes to preserve moisture.

Thick or Coarse Hair

Wash · Every Two to Three Days

Thick hair sits between fine and curly in most of its needs, able to go a little longer between washes than fine hair without looking oily, while still benefiting from a substantial conditioner to manage its volume and reduce frizz. Deep conditioning treatments once a week or so tend to pay off more visibly here than in any other type, since there is simply more hair for a treatment to noticeably improve.

Colour-Treated Hair

Wash · As Infrequently as Comfortable, Always Cool Water

Colour treatment raises hair's porosity, meaning it loses moisture and colour pigment more readily than untreated hair, which makes washing frequency and water temperature more important here than for any other type. Cooler water and a colour-safe, sulphate-free shampoo slow fading noticeably, and stretching the gap between washes, whatever your natural type would otherwise suggest, protects both colour vibrancy and the hair's condition.

The order that actually works

Regardless of type, the same basic sequence applies: shampoo the scalp rather than the lengths, since that is where oil and product buildup genuinely collect, let the rinse water carry cleansing down through the rest of the hair on its way out, then condition the mid-lengths and ends, where dryness actually lives, rather than the roots. Leave-in treatments, oils and styling products go on last, on damp rather than dripping hair, since hair that is too wet will simply dilute whatever you apply.

The mistakes that undo a good routine

Washing on a fixed daily schedule regardless of type is the most common mistake, since it is a habit built for fine hair being applied to hair that needs far less frequent washing to thrive. Skipping conditioner on curly or coarse hair to save time removes the step doing the most actual work. Using very hot water strips natural oils and fades colour faster on every hair type, and applying heavy product to fine hair roots causes the flatness people are usually trying to avoid in the first place. Matching the routine to the type, rather than to habit, resolves nearly all of it.

Quick questions

Is washing hair every day actually bad for it?

It depends entirely on type. Daily washing suits fine, oil-prone hair reasonably well, but the same schedule applied to curly, coily or colour-treated hair tends to cause dryness and, for colour-treated hair, noticeably faster fading. There is no universal answer, only a right answer for your specific hair.

What is co-washing and is it worth trying?

Co-washing means cleansing with conditioner instead of shampoo, and it is most useful for curly and coily hair between full shampoo washes, since it removes light buildup without stripping the moisture those hair types depend on. It generally does not suit fine hair well, where it can leave hair looking weighed down.

Does cold water really help hair colour last longer?

Yes, meaningfully so. Hot water opens the hair cuticle further and allows colour pigment to wash out faster, while cool water helps keep the cuticle closed, slowing both colour fade and general moisture loss. It is one of the simplest, lowest-effort changes for anyone with colour-treated hair.
