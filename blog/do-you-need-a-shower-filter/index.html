Do You Need a Shower Filter? Chlorine, Hard Water and Hair Explained

19 July 2026 · 9 minute read

Shower filters get sold as a fix for almost every hair and skin complaint at once, which oversells what the technology inside them can actually do. A shower filter is genuinely good at one thing: reducing chlorine and similar chemicals from the water hitting your skin and hair. It does not soften hard water, and confusing the two is the single biggest reason people end up disappointed after buying one.

What is actually in your water

Tap water in most areas carries chlorine or a related compound called chloramine, added deliberately to keep the water supply free of bacteria on its journey to your home. That same chlorine can strip natural oils from skin and hair, leaving both feeling drier than they would with unchlorinated water, and it can fade colour-treated hair faster over repeated exposure.

Separately, and unrelated to chlorine, many areas also have hard water, meaning a higher mineral content, mainly calcium and magnesium, picked up as water passes through certain types of rock before reaching the supply. Hard water is why kettles and shower screens develop white limescale over time, and the same minerals can leave hair feeling coated or heavier and skin feeling tight after washing.

What a standard shower filter actually removes

What It Removes Well

Chlorine, Chloramine, Some Sediment

Most shower filters use carbon or a metal alloy called KDF, sometimes both together, and this media is genuinely effective at reducing chlorine and chloramine levels along with some sediment and heavy metals as water passes through it. This is the part of the marketing that holds up under scrutiny.

What It Does Not Remove

Calcium and Magnesium, the Cause of Hard Water

Standard carbon and KDF filters are not built to remove dissolved minerals, so a shower filter will not soften hard water in any meaningful way, regardless of what packaging in this category sometimes implies. Genuinely softening water requires an ion-exchange system, typically a whole-house softener, a larger and more involved installation than a shower head attachment.

So who actually benefits

If your main complaint is hair or skin that feels dry, tight or irritated after showering, and your water supply uses standard chlorine treatment, which covers most of the country, a shower filter targets the right thing and a reasonable share of users notice a genuine difference in how skin and hair feel. Colour-treated hair in particular tends to hold its colour a little longer with reduced chlorine exposure, since chlorine is one of several factors that speeds fading.

If your main complaint is limescale buildup, hair that feels coated no matter what you wash it with, or a shower screen constantly covered in white residue, a standard shower filter will not solve it, since that is a hardness problem rather than a chlorine problem. A dedicated water softening system, or at minimum a filter specifically rated for scale reduction alongside chlorine, is the honest answer for that particular complaint.

How to tell if a filter is genuinely helping

Give any filter a proper trial of several weeks rather than judging after one shower, since skin and hair take time to show a real difference either way. Pay attention to whether hair feels less dry at the ends and whether skin feels less tight immediately after washing, since those are the effects chlorine reduction is most likely to produce. A drop in the faint chemical smell some tap water carries, particularly noticeable in a hot, enclosed shower, is another reasonable sign the filter is doing its job.

Maintenance and cartridge life

Filter media loses effectiveness gradually rather than stopping abruptly, so a cartridge past its recommended life will still let water through, just with less chlorine actually removed. Most cartridges are rated for a set number of months or a rough volume of water, typically printed on the packaging, and replacing on that schedule rather than waiting for a visible problem keeps the filter doing its actual job throughout its life.

Worth Knowing

If you are unsure whether your area has hard water, a call to your local water supplier will usually confirm it, and many publish hardness levels directly on their website. Knowing which problem you actually have, chlorine or hardness, is the difference between buying the right product the first time and being disappointed by the wrong one.

The mistakes worth avoiding

Buying a shower filter expecting it to fix limescale and hard water symptoms is the most common disappointment in this category, and it comes from marketing blurring a distinction that genuinely matters. Forgetting to replace the cartridge on schedule leaves a filter doing far less than its rating suggests while looking identical to a fresh one. Assuming a single shower will reveal whether a filter works skips the several weeks most people need before skin and hair show a real difference either way.

Quick questions

Will a shower filter soften hard water?

No. Standard shower filters use carbon or KDF media, which targets chlorine, chloramine and some sediment, not the dissolved calcium and magnesium that make water hard. Genuine softening needs an ion-exchange system, a different and larger piece of equipment entirely.

How long does a shower filter cartridge last?

Most are rated for a set number of months or a certain volume of water, printed on the packaging, commonly somewhere between two and six months depending on the model and how much the shower is used. Replacing on that schedule keeps the filter working at its rated effectiveness rather than quietly declining.

Can a shower filter help with a chlorine smell?

Yes, this is one of the areas where shower filters genuinely deliver, since reducing chlorine and chloramine directly reduces the chemical smell some tap water carries, particularly noticeable in a hot, steamy shower where the smell tends to concentrate.
