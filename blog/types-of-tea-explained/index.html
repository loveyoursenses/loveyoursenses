Types of Tea Explained: Black, Green, White, Oolong and Herbal

18 July 2026 · 6 minute read

Here is the fact that surprises most people starting out: black tea, green tea, white tea and oolong all come from the exact same plant. Every difference in colour, caffeine and flavour between them comes down to one thing, how the leaves are processed after picking, not which plant they grew on. Herbal tea, strictly speaking, is not tea at all, but that is a story for a moment.

Once that one fact clicks into place, the whole category stops feeling like a confusing wall of options and starts feeling like a single spectrum you can navigate on purpose.

One plant, one variable: oxidation

The plant is Camellia sinensis, and the variable that turns its leaves into black, green, white or oolong tea is oxidation, the process by which picked leaves darken and change flavour on exposure to air, in much the same way a cut apple browns. Leaves left to oxidize fully become black tea, bold and robust. Leaves that are heated quickly to stop oxidation almost entirely become green tea, lighter and grassier. Everything else on the spectrum sits somewhere between those two extremes, which is why the family resemblance is there if you know to look for it.

The main types

Black Tea

Oxidation · Full · Caffeine · Highest

Fully oxidized leaves give black tea its deep colour, robust flavour and the highest caffeine content of any true tea, making it the natural choice for a strong morning cup. It also holds up well to milk, which is why it forms the backbone of most everyday British tea blends.

Green Tea

Oxidation · Minimal · Caffeine · Low to Moderate

Leaves are heated shortly after picking to halt oxidation almost immediately, preserving a lighter, grassier, sometimes vegetal flavour and noticeably less caffeine than black tea. It rewards slightly cooler water than black tea, since boiling water tends to scald the delicate leaves and turn the flavour bitter.

White Tea

Oxidation · Very Light · Caffeine · Lowest of the True Teas

The least processed of all the true teas, made from young leaves and buds picked early and dried with minimal intervention. The resulting flavour is delicate and subtly sweet, and it is generally the gentlest option for anyone sensitive to caffeine who still wants tea rather than a herbal infusion.

Oolong Tea

Oxidation · Partial, Widely Variable · Caffeine · Moderate

Oolong sits deliberately between green and black, and the exact oxidation level varies enormously by producer, giving the category the widest flavour range of any tea type, from light and floral to dark and almost as robust as black tea. It is a good choice for anyone who wants to explore a single style with real variety inside it.

Herbal Tea (Tisanes)

Oxidation · Not Applicable · Caffeine · None

Chamomile, peppermint, rooibos and fruit blends contain no Camellia sinensis at all, which technically makes them tisanes rather than tea, though the word tea has stuck in everyday use. Naturally caffeine-free, they are the natural choice for an evening cup and the entire foundation of a proper wind-down ritual.

Matching tea to the time of day

Black tea's higher caffeine content makes it the strongest match for mornings, when a proper lift is usually welcome. Green and white teas suit the middle of the day well, offering a gentler caffeine boost without the jitters a second or third strong black tea can bring. Oolong works nicely as an afternoon transition, and herbal blends are the obvious choice as the day winds down, since removing caffeine from the equation entirely means they will not interfere with sleep however late you drink them.

The mistakes that ruin a good cup

Using boiling water on green or white tea is the single most common mistake, scalding the delicate leaves and pulling out bitterness rather than the flavour they are capable of, so a slightly cooler kettle or a short rest after boiling makes a real difference. Oversteeping is a close second across every type, since tea left too long releases increasing amounts of tannins and turns bitter regardless of how well it started. And treating all tea as interchangeable, reaching for a strong black tea at 9pm and wondering why sleep does not come easily, ignores caffeine content that varies enormously across the category.

Quick questions

Does green tea really have less caffeine than black tea?

Generally yes, though the gap is smaller than many people expect and both vary by leaf, brand and steeping time. As a rough guide, black tea sits highest among true teas, green and white lower, though a strong green tea can still contain more caffeine than a weakly brewed black one.

Is herbal tea actually tea?

Strictly speaking, no. True tea comes only from the Camellia sinensis plant, and herbal blends, made from dried flowers, herbs, fruit or roots, are more accurately called tisanes or infusions. The word tea has simply become the everyday term for both.

What water temperature should I use for tea?

Black tea and most herbal blends generally suit freshly boiled water. Green and white teas prefer it slightly cooler, roughly a minute off the boil, since full boiling water tends to scald their more delicate leaves and produce a bitter cup rather than a smooth one.
