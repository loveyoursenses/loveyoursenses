Essential Oils 101: How to Blend Your Own Signature Scent

18 July 2026 · 7 minute read

Essential oil blending sounds like it belongs to trained perfumers, but the underlying idea is the same three-layer structure we already use to explain fragrance families, just built by hand instead of bought in a bottle. Once you understand top, middle and base notes, mixing your own blend stops being guesswork and becomes a fairly simple, forgiving craft.

This guide covers the blending itself and the safety basics that matter before you start, not the specific health claims sometimes made about individual oils, which vary in evidence and are best researched separately from a genuinely reliable source if that is what you are after.

The three-layer structure

Just as a perfume unfolds over the course of a wear, a blended essential oil scent unfolds over the course of smelling it, and that unfolding comes from combining oils that evaporate at different speeds. Top notes are the lightest, most volatile oils, the first thing you smell and the first to fade, usually within the first fifteen to thirty minutes. Middle notes form the heart of the blend, emerging as the top notes fade and carrying the main character for the next hour or two. Base notes are the heaviest, slowest oils, providing depth and anchoring the whole blend, often still faintly present many hours later.

Top Notes

Examples · Citrus Oils, Peppermint, Eucalyptus

Bright and immediate, top notes are what draw you in first, but they fade fastest of the three layers. Bergamot, lemon, sweet orange and peppermint are common, popular choices, and a blend built entirely from top notes will smell lovely for a few minutes and then largely disappear.

Middle Notes

Examples · Lavender, Geranium, Rosemary, Clary Sage

The heart of the blend, middle notes carry most of the actual character once the top notes have faded, and they usually make up the largest proportion of a well-built blend by volume. Lavender is the classic starting point here for good reason, versatile and easy to pair with almost anything on this list.

Base Notes

Examples · Sandalwood, Cedarwood, Vanilla, Patchouli

Slow, deep and long-lasting, base notes anchor a blend and stop it feeling thin once the lighter layers have moved on. A little goes a long way here, since base notes tend to be the most concentrated and potent oils in the whole structure, easy to let dominate a blend if used too generously.

A simple ratio to start from

A commonly used and forgiving starting ratio is roughly thirty percent top notes, fifty percent middle notes and twenty percent base notes, adjusted afterward to taste once you have smelled the result. Start with a small batch, a handful of drops rather than a whole bottle, since blending is far easier to scale up once you like a combination than it is to fix an entire bottle that turned out wrong.

Dilution and carrier oils

Essential oils are highly concentrated and are not designed to touch skin undiluted. A carrier oil, sweet almond, jojoba and fractionated coconut oil are common, mild choices, dilutes the blend to a safe strength while barely affecting the scent itself. A commonly used general guideline for adult skin application is around a two percent dilution, roughly twelve drops of essential oil per thirty millilitres of carrier oil, though this varies by oil and intended use, so it is worth checking guidance specific to whichever oils you are working with.

Worth Knowing Before You Start

Always dilute essential oils before they touch skin, and patch test a new blend on a small area first. Several popular oils are considered unsuitable around pets, cats in particular are highly sensitive to many essential oils, so research any oil against a reliable pet safety source before diffusing around animals, and ensure the room is ventilated with an exit available. Certain oils are also generally advised against during pregnancy. If you are pregnant, nursing, managing a health condition, or unsure about a specific oil, checking with a qualified professional before use is the sensible, low-effort precaution.

The mistakes that spoil a good blend

Using too many base notes too early is the most common beginner mistake, since their strength is easy to underestimate and a blend can turn heavy and one-dimensional quickly. Skipping the small test batch and mixing a full bottle straight away wastes oil on a combination you have not actually confirmed you like. Applying an undiluted blend directly to skin risks irritation regardless of how naturally derived the ingredients are, and forgetting to let a blend rest for a day or two before final judgement means you are smelling the oils before they have properly combined, which changes the result more than most beginners expect.

Quick questions

Can I use essential oils directly on my skin without diluting them?

This is generally not recommended. Essential oils are highly concentrated and undiluted application carries a real risk of skin irritation or sensitisation over time, even with oils widely considered gentle. Diluting in a carrier oil at a sensible ratio, and patch testing first, is the safer standard approach.

How long does a homemade blend actually last?

A blend made with a carrier oil typically keeps for around six to twelve months, though this varies by the specific carrier oil used, since some are more prone to going rancid than others. Storing blends away from direct sunlight and heat, in a dark glass bottle where possible, extends their usable life noticeably.

Are essential oils safe to diffuse around children and pets?

Caution is warranted for both. Children generally need much greater dilution than adults if oils touch their skin at all, and several common oils are considered unsuitable around cats and some other pets. Ventilating the room, using a lower concentration than you would for yourself, and checking oil-specific guidance before diffusing around either group is the sensible approach.
