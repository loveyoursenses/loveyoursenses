Minimalist vs Maximalist Skincare: How Many Steps Do You Actually Need?

18 July 2026 · 6 minute read

Skincare content online tends to pull in two opposite directions at once. One half insists you need a cleanser, several serums, an eye cream, a moisturiser and a weekly mask to take care of your skin properly. The other half insists all of that is unnecessary noise and three honest products will do everything you need. Both camps are confident, both have real evidence behind them, and both are describing entirely reasonable routines for different people.

The truth is that neither approach is universally correct, because the right number of steps depends on your skin, your time and, frankly, how much you enjoy the process itself. This is not a question with one right answer, it is a question of matching a philosophy to your actual life.

Two philosophies, two different goals

Minimalist skincare, sometimes called skinimalism, is built on the idea that skin generally needs less interference than the industry suggests, and that a small number of well-chosen products, used consistently, outperforms a crowded shelf used inconsistently. Maximalist skincare treats the routine itself as a form of care and enjoyment, layering targeted products for specific concerns in a deliberate order, with the ritual of application counted as part of the benefit rather than a cost to be minimised.

The Minimalist Case

Core Belief · Less Interference, More Consistency

Fewer products means fewer chances to irritate skin with conflicting actives, fewer purchases to keep track of, and a routine simple enough to actually stick to every single day, which matters more than any individual product's marketing claims. Minimalists tend to argue that a cleanser, a moisturiser and daily sun protection cover most of what skin genuinely needs, with anything else added only to solve a specific, named problem.

The Maximalist Case

Core Belief · Layered, Targeted Care

Different concerns, hydration, texture, tone, ageing, respond to different ingredients, and maximalists argue that treating them all with one all-purpose moisturiser leaves real improvement on the table. The routine itself is also valued as a slow-down ritual, ten minutes of deliberate self-care rather than a task to rush through, which for many people is a genuine part of why the routine works.

How to tell which suits you

Start with time rather than skin type, because time is what actually determines whether a routine survives contact with a real morning. If ten minutes feels like a lot to spend on your face before coffee, a longer routine will simply get skipped on the busy days, which defeats its own purpose. If you already enjoy the process and look forward to it, a longer routine is not indulgent, it is sustainable precisely because you want to do it.

Skin sensitivity points the same direction as time pressure. Sensitive or reactive skin generally does better with fewer ingredients at once, since a shorter list makes it far easier to identify what caused a flare-up if one happens. Skin that tolerates actives well and has specific, named concerns to address has more to gain from a targeted, layered approach.

Building either version properly

A minimalist routine built well still covers the essentials rather than skipping them for the sake of simplicity. Morning: a gentle cleanser, a moisturiser suited to your skin type, and sun protection, non-negotiable regardless of which philosophy you follow. Evening: the same cleanser, and the same moisturiser, with a treatment product added only if you have a specific concern worth addressing, rather than because a routine felt incomplete without one.

A maximalist routine built well still respects an order, since applying products out of sequence can reduce how well any of them work. The general shape runs thinnest to thickest: cleanser, toner if used, water-based serums, thicker treatment products, eye cream, moisturiser, and sun protection in the morning or a facial oil at night to seal everything in. The discipline maximalism actually needs is patience, introducing one new product at a time so you can tell what is helping and what is simply adding cost.

The mistakes both camps make

Minimalists most often go wrong by skipping sun protection to keep the routine short, which undoes a great deal of the benefit the other steps were providing. Maximalists most often go wrong by adding several new active ingredients at once, making it nearly impossible to tell what is working or what is causing irritation if something goes wrong. Both camps make the same mistake in different directions when they copy someone else's exact routine rather than paying attention to how their own skin responds, since skin is genuinely individual and any routine, however well designed, needs adjusting to fit.

Quick questions

Is more expensive skincare always better than a simple routine?

No, price reflects ingredients, branding and packaging far more than it reflects effectiveness for your specific skin. A well-matched three-step minimalist routine can outperform an expensive, mismatched eight-step one, since consistency and suitability matter more than either the price tag or the step count.

Can I mix minimalist and maximalist approaches?

Very much so, and most real routines land somewhere between the two rather than at either extreme. A common pattern is a minimalist morning, quick and protective before the day starts, paired with a slightly longer, more deliberate evening routine when there is more time to enjoy the process.

How long should I trial a new routine before judging it?

Skin cell turnover means most changes take around four to six weeks to become visible, so judging a routine after a few days rarely reflects what it is actually capable of. Introduce changes one at a time within that window so you can tell what is genuinely responsible for any improvement.
