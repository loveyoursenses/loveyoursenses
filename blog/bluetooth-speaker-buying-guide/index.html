How to Choose a Bluetooth Speaker: Sound, Battery Life and Waterproofing Explained

18 July 2026 · 6 minute read

A Bluetooth speaker sold for a garden barbecue and one sold for a bedside table are answering completely different questions, even when they sit next to each other in the same shop aisle. The spec sheets look similar enough to blur together, but the moment you name where the speaker will actually live, most of the confusing choices resolve themselves.

Three things decide whether a speaker suits your life: how loud and full it needs to sound, how much knocking about it needs to survive, and how long it needs to run before finding a plug again. Everything else on the box is detail layered on top of those three.

Size is a trade, not just a spec

Speaker sound is mostly a function of physical space. A larger body allows a larger driver and more internal volume for bass to develop properly, which is why a speaker that fits in one hand will rarely fill a garden the way a shoebox-sized one can. That is not a flaw to engineer around, it is the actual physics of moving air, so the honest question is how much size you are willing to carry for how much sound you want back.

Home and Desk

Priority · Sound Quality Over Portability

If the speaker mostly stays on one shelf or desk, you can spend your budget entirely on sound rather than on ruggedness or battery, since it will likely live plugged in or charged overnight regardless. Larger cabinet speakers built for a stationary life reward that choice with noticeably fuller bass and clearer detail than anything designed to survive a rucksack.

Outdoor and Portable

Priority · Battery Life and Durability

Picnics, gardens and walks call for a compact body, a genuinely long battery rather than an optimistic one, and a drop-resistant build, since portable speakers get knocked about in ways a desk speaker never will. Sound quality takes a small step back here in exchange for a size you will actually want to carry, which is the trade worth making for this use case.

Shower and Bathroom

Priority · A Genuine Waterproof Rating

This is the one context where waterproofing is not optional. Bathroom humidity and direct splashing rule out anything without a proper IPX rating, and a compact size that suction-cups or hangs somewhere steam will not loosen it matters more here than outright sound quality.

Party and Group Listening

Priority · Volume and Bass, Battery Second

For filling a room or a garden with people in it, raw volume and punchy bass matter more than subtlety, and many speakers in this category can pair with a second unit for stereo sound or genuinely fill a larger space. Battery life still counts, but a party speaker is more likely to be near a plug than a walking one, so it earns a lower priority here.

What the numbers on the box actually mean

Waterproof ratings follow an IPX scale, and the second number is the one that matters for speakers. IPX4 handles splashes from any direction, enough for light rain or being near a pool. IPX7 means the speaker can survive full submersion in shallow water for a set time, which is the rating worth looking for if it is genuinely coming into the shower or the bath. Anything lower than IPX4 is really only dust-resistant or lightly weather-resistant, so check the number rather than trusting the word waterproof on its own, since marketing language is looser than the rating underneath it.

Battery life claims are usually measured at a moderate, unrealistic volume in ideal conditions, so treat the advertised number as a ceiling rather than a promise. Playing at party volume, using Bluetooth over a longer range, or running in cold weather will all shorten real-world runtime, sometimes considerably.

The mistakes that waste the most money

Buying a small, stylish speaker for outdoor group listening and being disappointed by the bass is one of the most common regrets in this category, since it is really a size problem dressed up as a sound quality one. Trusting the word waterproof without checking the IPX number is a close second. Believing the advertised battery life at face value, then being caught out on a long day out, rounds out the list. Match the speaker's built purpose to where it will actually live and most of these problems do not arise in the first place.

Quick questions

Does a bigger speaker always sound better?

Generally yes for bass and overall fullness, since more internal space allows a larger driver to move more air. Design and driver quality can narrow that gap considerably, but as a rule of thumb, size correlates with sound more reliably than almost any other spec on the box.

Can I really take an IPX7 speaker in the shower?

An IPX7 rating covers temporary submersion in shallow water, which comfortably covers shower splashes and steam. It is not designed for being fully dunked in a bath or pool for extended periods, so check the specific depth and time limit the manufacturer states rather than assuming all IPX7 ratings are identical.

Why does my speaker sound worse outdoors than it did in the shop?

Open air has nothing to bounce sound off, so bass in particular loses the reinforcement that walls and ceilings give it indoors. This is normal and not a fault, and it is one reason party-oriented outdoor speakers are tuned with extra bass to compensate for exactly this effect.
