How to Build a Body Care Routine: The Steps Most People Skip

18 July 2026 · 6 minute read

Most of us have a skincare routine for our face and no routine at all for the rest of the body beyond soap and whatever moisturiser happens to be in the cupboard. That gap is not really about laziness, it is about attention. Face skin gets a mirror and a morning ritual built around it, while body skin gets ninety seconds in the shower and is expected to look after itself.

The good news is that a proper body care routine takes very little extra time to build once you know the shape of it, and the difference it makes, softer skin, fewer rough patches, a moment of actual care in an otherwise rushed day, tends to be noticeable within a couple of weeks.

Why body skin needs its own routine

Body skin is thicker than facial skin in most places but far more exposed to friction, from clothing, from towels, from simply being sat on for a third of the day, and it produces fewer natural oils in areas like the shins and forearms than the face does. That combination is exactly why elbows, knees and lower legs tend to go dry and rough while the face, treated with a proper routine, stays comfortable. The fix is not complicated, it is simply giving the body the same basic sequence the face already gets.

The routine, step by step

1. Cleanse

Daily · The Foundation Step

A gentle, non-stripping body wash or bar does most of the work here, and hot water is worth resisting more than it seems, since very hot showers strip natural oils faster than warm ones and leave skin drier by the time you step out. This step needs no more thought than most people already give it, the goal is simply not undoing the good the later steps will do.

2. Exfoliate

Two to Three Times a Week · The Step Most People Skip

Dead skin cells build up on the body just as they do on the face, and left alone they make skin look dull and stop moisturiser absorbing properly. A body scrub, an exfoliating mitt or a simple dry brush before showering all do the job, focused gently on rougher areas like elbows, knees and heels. More is not better here, over-exfoliating irritates skin rather than improving it, so two or three times a week is the right frequency for most people.

3. Moisturise

Daily, Ideally on Damp Skin · The Step That Actually Locks Everything In

Applying body lotion or oil within a few minutes of stepping out of the shower, while skin is still slightly damp, helps trap the water already on the skin rather than letting it evaporate first. A richer cream or body butter suits drier areas and colder months, while a lighter lotion works better in summer or for those who prefer a less heavy feel. This is also the step where scent becomes part of the ritual rather than an afterthought, and it is worth choosing one you genuinely enjoy.

The extras worth adding, if you have the time

Hand and foot cream deserve a specific mention, since hands are washed more often than almost any other part of the body and dry out faster as a result, while feet carry weight all day and rarely get moisturiser at all. A weekly hand cream applied before bed and a foot cream a few times a week close a gap most routines leave entirely open. Body oil layered over lotion, or used alone on very dry skin, adds a final seal and a pleasant scent element that turns a functional routine into something closer to a ritual.

The mistakes that undo good intentions

Skipping moisturiser on non-visible areas, the backs of arms, shins, the tops of feet, is one of the most common gaps, simply because those areas are rarely looked at closely. Exfoliating too aggressively or too often irritates skin rather than smoothing it, and very hot showers undo much of the good the rest of the routine is doing. The last common mistake is treating body care as an occasional treat rather than a daily habit, when consistency, applied gently and often, is what actually produces visible results over a few weeks.

Quick questions

How often should I exfoliate my body?

Two to three times a week suits most skin types. More frequent exfoliation can irritate skin and damage its natural barrier rather than improving texture, so it is worth resisting the urge to overdo it even on noticeably rough patches.

Does body lotion actually work better on damp skin?

Yes, applying moisturiser within a few minutes of showering helps trap the water still sitting on the skin's surface, which is more effective than applying the same product to fully dry skin later. It is a small timing change that makes a real difference to how hydrated skin feels through the day.

Do I need different products for different seasons?

Many people do. Skin tends to need richer, heavier moisturiser in winter when air is drier and central heating pulls moisture out, while a lighter lotion often feels more comfortable in summer heat and humidity. Adjusting with the seasons rather than sticking to one product year round tends to serve skin better.
