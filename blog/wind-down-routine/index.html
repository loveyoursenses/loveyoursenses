How to Build a Wind-Down Routine That Actually Sends You to Sleep

18 July 2026 · 6 minute read

You cannot fall asleep on command. Sleep is not an action you perform but an outcome you arrange, and the arranging happens in the hour before bed, not in the moment your head touches the pillow. Most of us spend that hour doing the exact opposite: bright rooms, busy screens, one more episode, and then we lie down and wonder why the brain will not change gear.

There is even a name for the worst version of it. Revenge bedtime procrastination is the habit of staying up too late to claw back personal time from a busy day, and it is startlingly common. A wind-down routine is the antidote, because it turns that final hour into time that genuinely feels like yours while quietly preparing you for sleep at the same time.

Why a routine works when willpower does not

The brain is a pattern machine. Repeat the same sequence of cues each evening and it learns to treat the sequence itself as the signal, so that by the third or fourth step your body is already dimming its own lights. You have run the morning version of this for years, the shower, kettle and radio that wake you up without any effort at all. A wind-down routine is simply the same machinery pointed the other way.

Two pieces of biology do the heavy lifting. Falling light tells the brain to release melatonin, the hormone that opens the door to sleep, which is why dimming the house matters more than any product you could buy. And a gentle drop in body temperature is one of the strongest sleep signals there is, something you can trigger deliberately, as you will see in a moment. Everything that follows leans on those two levers, plus a third one: familiarity.

Build the hour one sense at a time

Because this is Love Your Senses, we build the hour the way we build everything, one sense at a time. You do not need all five every night. Pick the two or three that suit your life and keep them consistent, since consistency is where the power lives.

Sight

The Move · Lights Fall First

Dim the whole house an hour before bed, not just the bedroom. Switch from overheads to lamps, favour warm bulbs, and let the light physically descend to low sources rather than ceiling glare. Screens are the awkward part, and the honest advice is that what you watch matters as much as the glow, so a calm programme on a dim screen beats an hour of doomscrolling with the brightness turned down. The gold standard is a bedroom your phone does not sleep in. To get the whole room right, light and sound included, our bedroom atmosphere quiz maps it in a couple of minutes.

Sound

The Move · Consistent Beats Silent

Total silence is brittle, because a single creak or car door cuts straight through it. A gentle, steady bed of sound, rain audio, a slow ambient playlist, even a quiet fan, masks those spikes and gives a racing mind something soft to hold onto. Audiobooks and calm podcasts at low volume work beautifully as the screen replacement, with a sleep timer set so they switch themselves off after you have gone.

Smell

The Move · Train the Cue

Scent is the most underused tool in the sleep kit, because the nose is wired straight into memory. Choose one calming scent, and lavender is popular for good reason, then use it only at wind-down time, as a pillow spray, a diffuser or a candle you blow out well before you drift. Within a few weeks the scent alone starts telling your brain what happens next. We compared the formats honestly in our candles, diffusers and room sprays guide if you are choosing one.

Touch

The Move · Warm Then Cool

Here is the temperature trick. A warm bath or shower an hour or two before bed warms the skin, and it is the cooling that follows which pulls your core temperature down and makes you genuinely sleepy, so the timing matters more than the soak. Then keep the bedroom itself cool, around eighteen degrees suits most people, with bedding that lets you regulate rather than swelter. Comfort under your head counts just as much, and if yours has gone flat, our pillow quiz will find its replacement.

Taste

The Move · Kind to Tomorrow

The last hour is a poor time for heavy food, late caffeine or a nightcap. Caffeine lingers far longer than it feels like it does, so an afternoon cut-off protects the evening, and alcohol is the great pretender, helping you fall asleep faster and then fragmenting the second half of the night. What earns its place is a warm, caffeine-free drink treated as a small ritual, and chamomile, rooibos and other herbal blends were made for exactly this job. Our tea quiz will match you to yours.

What an hour actually looks like

Here is one shape it might take, starting sixty minutes out. The overheads go off and the lamps come on, and the kettle goes on with them. You carry the tea somewhere comfortable, start the rain playlist or tonight's chapter of an audiobook, and spend twenty unhurried minutes with it. A warm shower follows, and while your body cools you do the small closing ceremonies of the day: tomorrow's list scribbled down so your head does not have to hold it, the phone onto its charger outside the bedroom, one spray of lavender on the pillow. By the time you lie down, every sense has already been told the same thing.

Do not treat that as a script. Treat it as a shape, and swap any element for one that fits your life. The only rule is that tonight's version looks like last night's.

Making it stick

Start with twenty minutes rather than a full hour, because a routine you keep beats a routine you admire. Run the steps in the same order each night, since order is what the brain memorises, and hold your bedtime roughly steady at weekends, because a Monday body clock cannot be argued with. If you are still wide awake twenty minutes after lying down, get up, keep the lights low and do something genuinely dull until sleepiness returns, which protects the bed's job of meaning sleep and nothing else.

And if sleep stays difficult for weeks no matter what you change, that is a conversation for your GP rather than a candle, and there is no shame at all in having it.

Quick questions

How long should a wind-down routine be?

Twenty minutes is enough to work and an hour is lovely if you have it. Consistency matters far more than length, because the routine's power comes from repetition rather than duration. Start small and let it grow into the space your evening allows.

Do I really have to give up screens before bed?

Not entirely, and pretending otherwise is why so much sleep advice gets ignored. The stimulation matters as much as the light, so calm content on a dim screen is a reasonable compromise, while endless feeds and anything with a scoreline are the true enemies. Keeping the phone out of arm's reach of the bed is the single change that pays the most.

What if my mind races the moment the lights go off?

Give the racing somewhere else to happen. A two-minute brain dump onto paper earlier in the evening, everything you are holding, tomorrow's tasks included, tells your head it is safe to put things down. If the racing continues night after night and starts affecting your days, mention it to your GP, because persistent sleep trouble deserves to be taken seriously.
