White Noise, Pink Noise or Brown Noise: Which Helps You Sleep?

19 July 2026 · 8 minute read

Our wind-down routine guide makes the case for consistent background sound over true silence, since a steady sound bed masks the small spikes, a creak, a passing car, that otherwise pull a tired mind back toward alertness. What that guide does not cover is which colour of noise actually does that job best, and the answer depends more on personal comfort than most product pages let on.

The three colours explained

White Noise

Equal Energy Across Every Frequency

White noise plays every audible frequency at roughly equal intensity, which produces the familiar static-like hiss most people picture first when they hear the term. It is the most effective at masking sudden or varied sounds, since it covers the full range those sounds might occupy, but some listeners find its higher frequencies a little sharp for extended listening.

Pink Noise

Softer, More Balanced, Weighted Toward Lower Tones

Pink noise reduces the intensity of higher frequencies relative to white noise, producing a softer, deeper sound closer to steady rainfall or wind through leaves. Many people find it more pleasant for extended listening than white noise, and it still masks background sound effectively while sitting more comfortably in the ear over a full night.

Brown Noise

Deepest and Most Rumbling of the Three

Brown noise pushes even further toward low frequencies than pink noise, producing a deep, rumbling quality closer to distant thunder or a strong waterfall. It suits listeners who find white or pink noise too bright or hissy, though its low, heavy quality is not to everyone's taste.

Why constant sound helps in the first place

None of the three colours are inherently better at helping you fall asleep. What they share is consistency, a steady, unchanging sound that gives the ear something predictable to settle into. That consistency works by masking, covering smaller, irregular noises that would otherwise catch attention and pull focus back toward wakefulness. A perfectly silent room actually makes those small sounds more noticeable by contrast, which is why many light sleepers find true silence harder to sleep through than a steady hum.

Choosing between the three colours comes down to comfort more than performance. Try each for a few nights and notice which one you stop consciously hearing the fastest, since that is the sign your brain has accepted it as background rather than something worth attending to.

Standalone machine or app and speaker

A dedicated white noise machine offers a simple, single-purpose device with no phone screen glowing nearby and usually a genuine timer or all-night continuous mode built in. A phone app through a speaker costs nothing extra if you already own both, offers a wider range of sound options to experiment with, and comes with the trade-off of a phone sitting closer to the bed than sleep hygiene advice generally recommends. Either genuinely works, and the choice mostly comes down to whether you would rather keep your phone out of the bedroom entirely.

A Genuine Volume Safety Note

White noise machines are commonly used in nurseries, and general safety guidance from paediatric health bodies suggests keeping the volume moderate, comparable to a soft shower running rather than close to a vacuum cleaner, and positioning the machine at least a couple of metres from a cot rather than right beside it. If you are using one for a baby, check current guidance from a trusted health source and the manufacturer's own instructions rather than relying on this article alone.

The mistakes worth avoiding

Running any colour of noise at a high volume assuming louder masks better usually backfires, since a sound loud enough to be genuinely attention-grabbing works against the calm, background effect that makes the whole approach work. Switching colours every night rather than sticking with one long enough to judge it fairly makes it hard to know what actually suits you. Placing a machine, particularly in a nursery, too close to where someone sleeps ignores the volume guidance worth following for anyone, not just infants.

Quick questions

Is pink noise scientifically better for sleep than white noise?

Neither colour has been shown to reliably outperform the other for most people, and comfort tends to matter more than any measurable difference. Pink noise's softer, less hissy quality makes it more pleasant for many listeners over a full night, which is often the real reason it gets preferred rather than a proven sleep advantage.

Can white noise stop working over time?

Some people do notice a sound they once found masking becomes more noticeable again after months of nightly use, similar to how a scent can stop registering once you are used to it. Switching between two colours occasionally, rather than using the exact same sound every single night indefinitely, can help keep the masking effect working.

What volume is safe for a white noise machine near a baby?

General guidance suggests keeping the machine at a moderate volume, roughly comparable to a soft shower, and positioned a reasonable distance from the cot rather than right beside it. Always check current advice from a trusted health source and the specific manufacturer's instructions for the exact machine you are using.
